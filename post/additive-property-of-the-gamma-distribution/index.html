Additive Property of the Gamma Distribution

Last updated on Sep 3, 2019 4 min read Bayesian Nonparametrics

While learning Bayesian Nonparametric methodology, I found a few properties useful in understanding the way Dirichlet Process Priors worked (this is a prior used very commonly in the field (Ferguson 1973, @antoniak1974mixtures)) . This post will be one in a series that describes each of the aforementioned properties and provides simple proofs/examples of the same.

As the title suggests, we start with the Gamma distribution. We use the shape and rate parameterization of the distribution (as explained here). Without loss of generality in this case, we also set the rate parameter $ = 1$ for all random variables unless otherwise mentioned. We want to prove the additive property of the gamma distribution:

\[\begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i \sim Gamma(\sum_{i=1}^{N}\alpha_i, 1) \nonumber \\ Where \, X_i \sim Gamma(\alpha_i, 1), \, \, i \in \{1,2, \dots, N\} \tag{1} \end{align}\]

Where \[\begin{align} Gamma(\alpha_i, 1) = f_{X_i}(x; \alpha_i) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_i)} x^{(\alpha_i -1)} e^{-x} \tag{2} \end{align}\]

Importantly, the \(X_i\)’s are independent. We prove the identity by induction. For \(N = 1\), the property in (1) holds trivially. For \(N=2\), we set about deriving this from first principles. Let \(Y = X_1 + X_2\). We try to get the distribution of \(Y\). The following holds for \(Y\):

\[\begin{align} P(Y \le y) & = P(X_1 + X_2 \le y) \nonumber \\ \implies P(Y \le y) & = P(X_1 \le y - X_2) \nonumber \\ \implies P(Y \le y) & = P(X_1 \le y - X_2 | X_2)P(X_2) \tag{3} \end{align}\]

The expression in (3) is a double integral, one to vary \(X_2\) over the range of values it can possibly take: \((0, \infty)\), and the other to vary \(X_1\) from \((0, y-X_2)\). This integral takes the form below:

\[\begin{align} P(Y \le y) & = P(X_1 \le y - X_2 | X_2)P(X_2) = \int_{0}^{\infty} \left( \int_{0}^{y-x_2} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} x_{1}^{(\alpha_1 -1)} e^{-x_1} dX_1 \right) \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} x_{2}^{(\alpha_2 -1)} e^{-x_2} dX_2 \tag{4} \end{align}\]

Note also, that the density of \(Y\) is given by: \[\begin{align} f_{Y}(y) & = \frac{dP(Y \le y)}{dy} \tag{5} \end{align}\]

Applying the identity in (5) to the integral in (4) and using the Leibniz integral rule, we get the following:

\[\begin{align} f_{Y}(y) & = \frac{d}{dy} \int_{0}^{\infty} \left( \int_{0}^{y-x_2} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} x_{1}^{(\alpha_1 -1)} e^{-x_1} dX_1 \right) \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} x_{2}^{(\alpha_2 -1)} e^{-x_2} dX_2 \nonumber \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = \int_{0}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} (y-x_2)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} e^{-(y-x_2)} \right) \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} x_{2}^{(\alpha_2 -1)} e^{-x_2} dX_2 \nonumber \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} (y-x_2)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} x_{2}^{(\alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} dX_2 \tag{6} \end{align}\]

We now attempt to convert the integral in (6) into a form that is well known. We set \(Z = \frac{X_2}{y}\), which gives us \(dX_2 = ydZ\), and also note that \(Z\) can take the range of values bounded by the interval \((0,1)\) (\(Z \rightarrow 0\) as \(X_2 \rightarrow 0\) and \(Z \rightarrow 1\) as \(X_2 \rightarrow \infty\), since \(Y \rightarrow \infty\) too). Replacing \(X_2\) with \(Z\) in (6), we get:

\[\begin{align} f_{Y}(y) & = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} (y- yz)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} (yz)^{(\alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} ydZ \nonumber \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} y^{(\alpha_1 -1)} (1- z)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} y^{(\alpha_2 -1)} z^{(\alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} ydZ \nonumber \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = \int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} y^{(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} (1- z)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} z^{(\alpha_2 -1)} dZ \nonumber \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} y^{(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} \int_{0}^{1} (1- z)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} z^{(\alpha_2 -1)} dZ \tag{7} \end{align}\]

The integral in (7) is the Beta function, whose value is given below:

\[\begin{align} \int_{0}^{1} (1- z)^{(\alpha_1 -1)} z^{(\alpha_2 -1)} dZ = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha_1)\Gamma(\alpha_2)}{\Gamma(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2)} \tag{8} \end{align}\]

Substituting (8) back into (7), we get: \[\begin{align} f_{Y}(y) & = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1)} \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_2)} y^{(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha_1)\Gamma(\alpha_2)}{\Gamma(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2)}\nonumber \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2)} y^{(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 -1)} e^{-y} \tag{9} \\ \implies f_{Y}(y) & = Gamma(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, 1) \nonumber \end{align}\]

From (9), we have now proved that the property in (1) holds for \(N=2\). We now assume this is true for \(N\) and show that this property holds for \(N+1\). Now, let \(Y = \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i\), which gives us the following identities:

\[\begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^{N+1} X_i & = Y + X_{N+1} \tag{10} \\ Y & \sim Gamma(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i,1) \tag{11} \end{align}\]

(11) follows from the induction assumption. Since the property in (1) is true for \(N=2\), we combine this with the identities in (10) & (11) to get:

\[\begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^{N+1} X_i & = Y + X_{N+1} \sim Gamma(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i + \alpha_{N+1},1) \nonumber \\ \implies \sum_{i=1}^{N+1} X_i & \sim Gamma(\sum_{i=1}^{N+1} \alpha_i,1) \tag{12} \end{align}\]

(12) shows that the property in (1) is true for \(N+1\), completing the proof.

References

Antoniak, Charles E. 1974. “Mixtures of Dirichlet Processes with Applications to Bayesian Nonparametric Problems.” The Annals of Statistics, 1152–74.

Ferguson, Thomas S. 1973. “A Bayesian Analysis of Some Nonparametric Problems.” The Annals of Statistics, 209–30.
